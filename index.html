Kočakote kočakote

Kočakote is an experimental, constructed language made by Jonatan Nordentoft in 2016 with the intent of creating a condensed means mess of communication by getting rid of unnecessary fillers such as prepositions and making pronunciation as easy as possible while still keeping an interesting sound to the language. Kočakote is not meant to be used seriously, but I won't stop you if you do. Below should be everything you need to know, but if not, feel free to contact me.

    Writing System & Sounds kosinuča šøsisinoto tolo kosonosi

    Kočakote can be written either using its own syllabary or romanized using the Latin alphabet. You can download a ttf of the computer font to use here. It's licensed under the SIL Open Font License v1.1.

    Every character in the Kočakote syllabary consist of a glyph at the top, the consonant, and a line at the bottom, the vowel. Kočakote is a CV language, meaning that the consonant is pronounced first. Words are separated by breaking the line—there's no need to create a space inbetween them as in the computer font.

    Kočakote has a fairly small inventory consisting of 10 consonants and 8 vowels, meaning that mastering the syllabary should take very little time!

    You can make a syllable of every possible combination of a consonant and a vowel, only exception to this rule is the “consonant” of ' (t͡s). This character is used as an accent after a letter used to indicate affixes. Due to Kočakote's nature as a CV language, you must put a vowel after it of your choice. Just any one you like. I.e. kone'løna'četo (kone'løna'četo, “opposite of free place” meaning prison) could be pronounced as konet͡silœnat͡sut͡ʃeto.

    There's no such thing as uppercase letters in Kočakote. Below is the Kočakote translation of “beautiful language” in both writing systems for comparison.

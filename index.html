Kočakote kočakote

Kočakote is an experimental, constructed language made by Jonatan Nordentoft in 2016 with the intent of creating a condensed means mess of communication by getting rid of unnecessary fillers such as prepositions and making pronunciation as easy as possible while still keeping an interesting sound to the language. Kočakote is not meant to be used seriously, but I won't stop you if you do. Below should be everything you need to know, but if not, feel free to contact me.

Please note that this specification is a work in progress and more information will be added later.

    Writing System & Sounds kosinuča šøsisinoto tolo kosonosi

    Kočakote can be written either using its own syllabary or romanized using the Latin alphabet. You can download a ttf of the computer font to use here. It's licensed under the SIL Open Font License v1.1.

    Every character in the Kočakote syllabary consist of a glyph at the top, the consonant, and a line at the bottom, the vowel. Kočakote is a CV language, meaning that the consonant is pronounced first. Words are separated by breaking the line—there's no need to create a space inbetween words or punctuation as in the computer font.

    Kočakote has a fairly small inventory consisting of 10 consonants and 8 vowels, meaning that mastering the syllabary should take very little time!

    You can make a syllable of every possible combination of a consonant and a vowel, only exception to this rule is the “consonant” of ' (t͡s). This character is used as an accent after a letter used to indicate affixes. Due to Kočakote's nature as a CV language, you must put a vowel after it of your choice. Just any one you like. I.e. kone'løna'četo (kone'løna'četo, “opposite of free place” meaning prison) could be pronounced as konet͡silœnat͡sut͡ʃeto.

    There's no such thing as uppercase letters in Kočakote. Below is the Kočakote translation of “beautiful language” in both writing systems for comparison.

    ko li no ča ko bæ la sa
    ko li no ča ko bæ la sa

    Basic grammar kolakøtuče kobåkadø

    Kočakote has no specific word order; you put the most important information first, or just use whatever sounds the nicest. The semantics of all words are determined by their respective case. Most word classes use cases specified by the first syllable.

    Cases kokasosi

    Kočakote is a very case-heavy language with extremely few prepositions. The first syllable of a word determines its case. Below is a list of all cases and examples of their uses.

    ko Nominative

    The most common case in Kočakote is indisputably the nominative case. Words in the nominative case are the subjects or “agents” of an action (denoted by a cross). In example:

    English
    That person is me.
    Kočakote
    kotiši donika kotičo.

    be Accusative

    The accusative case is used for the direct object of an action (denoted by a triangle). In example:

    English
    I know them.
    Kočakote
    tikabuka bedočo.

    to Temporal

    The temporal case is used to indicate the time or date at which something happens (denoted by a squiggly line). In example:

    English
    It will happen on Friday at 5pm.
    Kočakote
    nøkånuso tolinuko datose točasiko.

    Notice how “5pm” is translated as “the 17th hour”.

    ne Desirative

    The desirative case is used to wish someone something. It's the difference between stating the fact that a good day is indeed a thing and actually wishing it for someone. In example:

    English
    All the best.
    Kočakote
    nečakote.

    sæ Opiniative

    The opiniative case is used to specify that something is according to someone, in accordance with something or the like (denoted by a squiggly line). In example:

    English
    In my opinion you're extremely beautiful.
    Kočakote
    sætičo dibæla'baka.

    ka Genitive

    The genitive case is used to specify ownership of something (denoted by a squiggly line). In example:

    English
    They have my money.
    Kočakote
    donaka betidæši kanenedø.

    Notice how “my money” is translated by the use of the possessive correlative and “money” is in the genitive case.

    na Benefactive

    The benefactive case is used to show that something is for the benefit of someone or something (denoted by a squiggly line). Equivalent to the English preposition “for” in most cases, but for example not when the meaning is “because of”. In example:

    English
    I'm beautiful for you.
    Kočakote
    tibælaka nadičo.

    ki Ablative relation

    The ablative relation case is used for to indicate that something is about or concerning something (denoted by a squiggly line). In example:

    English
    The book is about lingual dignity.
    Kočakote
    kotutuko denika kišåtanuče kilinodæ.

    så Durative

    The durative case is used to indicate that something took place for the duration of something (denoted by a squiggly line). In example:

    English
    The couple was kissing the entire night.
    Kočakote
    kokonučø dokisini såtutabi sånokočø.

    su Causal

    The causal case is used to indicate that something took place because of something (denoted by a squiggly line). In example:

    English
    He likes her because she's rich.
    Kočakote
    dosanaka bedočo donika sutikikæ.

    lå Allative

    The allative case is used to indicate movement towards something, both figuratively and literally (denoted by a squiggly line). In example:

    English
    I died on the way to the lighthouse.
    Kočakote
    tone'soso'tuki lånånæko šødonoko..

    se Apudessive

    The apudessive case is used to indicate a position next to something (denoted by a squiggly line). In example:

    English
    We met and exchanged packages on a bench next to the fountain in the park.
    Kočakote
    kinini'tuki tolo kitokanåtilaki bešæde detutoča sebanasukočø dibušočø.

    di Inessive

    The inessive case is used to indicate a position inside something (denoted by a squiggly line). In example:

    English
    We met and exchanged packages on a bench next to the fountain in the park.
    Kočakote
    kinini'tuki tolo kitokanåtilaki bešæde detutoča sebanasukočø dibušočø.

    de Superessive

    The superssive case is used to indicate a position on top of something (denoted by a squiggly line). In example:

    English
    We met and exchanged packages on a bench next to the fountain in the park.
    Kočakote
    kinini'tuki tolo kitokanåtilaki bešæde detutoča sebanasukočø dibušočø.

    šæ Potive

    The potive case is used to indicate something that happens for every something (denoted by a squiggly line). In example:

    English
    Beer costs 5 dollars per unit.
    Kočakote
    kobinoče lekotaka taså čokuničo'nišu šætenetoča.

    čo Measural

    The measural case is used to indicate some amount of some unit (denoted by a squiggly line). In example:

    English
    Beer costs 5 dollars per unit.
    Kočakote
    kobinoče lekotaka taså čokuničo'nišu šætenetoča.

    bi Instrumental

    The instrumental case is used to indicate that some action took place by the use of something else (denoted by a squiggly line). In example:

    English
    She collected berries with a pouch.
    Kočakote
    došæki benakæče bibatiča.

    da Antessive

    The antessive case is used to indicate that something took place in time before something else (denoted by a squiggly line). In example:

    English
    The parent found the container before the child.
    Kočakote
    kobatako dotoniki bešæčø datiliko.

    lø Essive

    The essive case is used to indicate that something took place in time after something else (denoted by a squiggly line). In example:

    English
    The child found the container after the parent.
    Kočakote
    kotiliko dotoniki bešæčø løbatako.

    šø Conjunctive

    The conjunctive case is used to create compound words. In example:

    English
    The Declaration of Human Rights
    Kočakote
    kotonosi šøladašu šødekačiko
